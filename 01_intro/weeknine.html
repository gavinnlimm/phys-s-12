Networking and IoT

1. Wireless Control (Microcontroller)

Week nine of our class promises to be a captivating and knowledge-packed session focused on ESP32 devices, wireless communication, and networking. The agenda begins with an Assignment Review at 12:00, providing students with valuable feedback on their recent work. At 12:30, we will dive into setting up ESP32 devices, enabling us to explore their capabilities and functionalities. The session will then move on to ESP-NOW Radio at 12:45, where we will learn about this communication protocol and its applications in wireless data transmission. Following this, at 1:00, we will delve into the world of Wired Communication, covering digital I/O and Wired Serial Communication. A comprehensive overview of WiFi and Bluetooth is scheduled for 1:15, providing insights into these essential wireless communication technologies. The session after the break at 1:45 will be dedicated to identifying the networking needs for our final projects, ensuring that we have a clear understanding of the requirements. The highlight of the week will be the Wireless Networking Workshop at 2:00, offering various options for exploration, including LAN Server setup, Remote Server using Firebase, HTTP Client for APIs and web scrapers, Bluetooth applications, tutorials from RandomNerd, and Arduino IoT Cloud exemplified by Faraz's example. This hands-on workshop will empower us with the skills and knowledge needed to design and implement wireless networking solutions for our projects, making week nine an engaging and practical experience.

First, I opened up Fusion 360, and crafted out a triangle. I then utilized the mirror and circular pattern tool to create the shape of the shuriken. After that, I crate construction lines in the middle of the shuriken, which would then be used to create the wedges in the shuriken, that would allow each individual shape to connect to one another. These wedges measure precisely 3.8mm in length.


After completing the design of the shuriken, I utilized the extruding tool to generate the thickness of the 2d object. This thickness (3.8mm) matched the thickness of the medium that we were using in the laser cutter: cardboard.
